If you're clearing out a closet in the OKC metro and a box of old toys surfaces, the easy move is the donate pile. Before it goes there, here's what's actually worth pulling aside — and how selling it for cash can take less effort than hauling it anywhere.

## The stuff that's worth a second look

You don't need a mint-in-box collection. The things buyers consistently want:

- **Action figures from the 80s, 90s, and 2000s** — Star Wars, TMNT, Power Rangers, WWF/WWE, G.I. Joe, Batman: the Animated Series. Loose is fine; complete-ish is better.
- **Playsets and vehicles** — even missing a piece or two. The Castle Grayskulls and Batcaves of the world hold real value.
- **Vintage lines your parents' generation played with** — He-Man, Transformers, My Little Pony, original Care Bears.
- **The weird stuff** — fast-food promo toys still in bags, oddball 90s electronics toys, McDonald's Happy Meal displays. Don't assume it's junk because it was free in 1994.

Condition matters, but less than people think. A played-with figure lot still has buyers; the trash truly is things that are broken, moldy, or heavily sun-faded.

## Why "sell it yourself" usually doesn't happen

You *could* list it all online: photograph every figure, research what it is (harder than it sounds with loose toys), price it, wait for buyers, ship it piece by piece. For a bin of mixed toys, that's genuinely hours of work — which is why the bin usually just goes back in the attic for another five years.
